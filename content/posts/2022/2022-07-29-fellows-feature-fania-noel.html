I am a Ph.D. candidate in Sociology, and I am an AfroFeminist organizer. I joined academia to think deeply about some questions, and this was a way to buy time for five years. When you have work and do activism, time is rare and something you need - so that is what led me to my Ph.D. Since I was already on that path of academia, I am still on the same topic, but from an analytical point of view, even though being an activist is already deeply analytical. This is more time to reflect on what we're doing as a Black organization, for instance.

For me, it's really easy because I am in academia and an activist in many groups. I founded and created journal reviews, and projects. My own practice is to create popular education where people meet, talk and radicalize their political consciousness. My own brand of work is to make organizations mobilize people, and put them on the street. Put them also in the position to think about their material conditions. So I think I am engaging in that in my own journey in academia by doing and being involved in the Gender and Sexuality students at the New School, by organizing panels for the public that are close to my area of work, but not my area of work. Writing also for mainstream and general publications. Talking with students and fellow Ph.D. students about what we can build together as a graduate conference, and participating in other conferences.

Engagement with the audience and public outside of my own area of practice is every day because I am really present in the socio major and a lot of public events and interning (?).

notes: >- alt: >- credit: >- - template: chapter title: >- What is the future that you are working towards in your practice? How do you keep this future in focus? position: 4 data: text: >-

The future, the creation that I think about a lot because the title is "Poem now: Afro Poetical reason (?)" - the reason, the future, the landscape the day after the end of the world is something persistent in my practice. I keep my focus on what I am thinking about being an ethical practice. How I can practice garian-telectualism, what Taran Nehto said (?). How I am doing the work as a step or break into the journey of revolutionary, thinking about how the work can be useful for the present knowledge, and how it can be useful for the present and future struggle. I am doing the work thinking it can help and hold community. And I am doing the work thinking about what kind of public scholar I want to become. It is really important to me. I will be an academic, but I wanted to become a public scholar. What I want to be, and what I am kind of, but I want to be a different type of. I am working on the ethics of what it is to be a Black feminist, an Afro feminist public scholar. In terms of the work you do, the way you interact with knowledge, with archive, with people, with the people you collect, with the interviewee. Also, the information you put in this space- in this violent space that academia can be, and how you are not participating in the casualization of knowledge. Thinking about the future is for me to think every day about how we engage and commit every day to an ethic, a Black feminist ethical of the practice of knowledge and scholarship.
